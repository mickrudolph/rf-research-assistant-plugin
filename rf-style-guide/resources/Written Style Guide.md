**Introduction and Table of Contents**

The RainFocus Style Guide is intended to ensure clarity and consistency in all written and published materials. It defines standards for voice, tone, grammar, punctuation, capitalization, and usage. Deploying and referencing a single source for style decisions helps us maintain accurate, uniform copy. 

Accuracy and consistency improve the effectiveness of communication with clients, prospects, employees, and other readers; streamlines the internal content creation and review process; and contributes to brand integrity and elevation by maintaining a polished, professional look and feel.

Although it is used most often by content creators and editors, the Style Guide is an informational resource for everyone. It is designed to work in tandem with [the Brand Style Guide](https://rainfocus.atlassian.net/wiki/spaces/MAR/pages/552665110/Brand+Guide+Design+Resources). 

Examples of correct and incorrect writing (indicated with bullet points) are included in each section. 

Official references: AP Stylebook, *Webster’s New World College Dictionary* 

For entries not listed in this style guide, defer to guidance in these official references. 

The guide is regularly maintained and updated. Contact [Jessica Lillian](mailto:jessica.lillian@rainfocus.com) with any questions or suggestions for additions.

**Writing About RainFocus: Trademarks and Brand**

**Trademarks**

Use the trademark (™) symbol with the first mention of RainFocus in a document (e.g., blog post, downloadable guide, sales sheet). Ensure the symbol is in superscript format. 

Use the trademark symbol with the first mention of Essential or any other products. Do not use the registered trademark (®) symbol in any instances.

For website content and other intellectual property, include a copyright line at the bottom or as appropriate. Use a space between the copyright symbol (©) and the year. 

**Additional Guidelines**  
When writing out the company name, always capitalize the R and the F. Never use “Rainfocus” or “rainfocus.”  Do not use a space between “Rain” and “Focus.” 

See [the Brand Style Guide](https://rainfocus.atlassian.net/wiki/spaces/MAR/pages/552665110/Brand+Guide+Design+Resources) for further guidance on official logo specs and usage. 

Because “RainFocus” can refer to either the company or the platform, ensure your intended meaning is clear and consistent. Do not use the word to refer to both the company and the platform in the same sentence.

* Founded in 2013, RainFocus is backed by several high-profile investors.  
* RainFocus includes several modules for end-to-end event management. 

The term "platform" is reserved only for RainFocus. Do not use “platform” to describe a competitor’s product; use “system” or “suite of products.” 

Refer to RainFocus as “we,” not “it.”

Capitalize branded terms, such as Essential, and names of modules. Do not capitalize generic descriptive product or feature names. 

* The Attendee Module offers several impressive features.   
* Check out the mobile app for more information. 

RainFocus INSIGHT is always written in all caps. Avoid writing “INSIGHT” without “RainFocus” except where there are multiple references within a single document.

RainFocus’ educational offering is written as RF Academy in all instances.

**General Principles**   
**(Adapted From the MailChimp Style Guide)**

**Write for all readers.** Depending on the type of content, some people will read every word you write. Others will scan headlines, first sentences, and other markers to find areas of interest. Help everyone read better and ensure they grasp the most important points by grouping related ideas together and using descriptive headers and subheaders.

**Target your message.** Create a hierarchy of information. Lead with the main point or the most important content in each paragraph, section, page, or other dividing unit.

**Be concise.** Use short words and sentences. Avoid unnecessary modifiers. See “Eliminating Redundancy and Wordiness” section. 

**Be specific.** Avoid vague language. 

**Be consistent.** Follow the copy patterns and style points outlined in this guide.

### **Tone**

Voice and tone are closely related. Although the company voice should be consistent throughout any type of writing, tone may vary depending on the type of writing and its audience. For example, a blog post written primarily for a general audience unfamiliar with the platform may use a different tone than a technical communication sent to specific subset of clients. Always consider your personas, their expectations for the type of content they are consuming, and their level of experience with the company and the platform. 

Generally, writing should be informative and relatable. Write objectively. The tone should always indicate a level of expertise that will help the reader feel confident that the information they are consuming is accurate and will help them solve a problem or answer a question. Add enough personality to show readers that the content was written by a real person. 

Language optimization (e.g. for search engines, greater social media visibility, improved email open rates) may be needed for some types of content, but writing should always remain natural. We should present ourselves as experienced, compassionate business partners.

**Additional Tone Qualities** 

**Professional.** We avoid humor, puns, pop culture references, slang, and any inappropriate language in our content. Content should never contain political viewpoints or any references that could be offensive or hurtful to any group, or damage the company’s reputation. 

**Understandable.** Different types of content may require different levels of technical detail. However, always aim for clarity. Avoid marketing jargon. Explain any important terms that your audience may not understand. Aim for relatability.

**Concise.** See the “Redundancies and Wordiness” section for examples and substitutions. 

**Definitive.** Introducing too many questions or overusing words such as “might” and “may” can make the writing feel uncertain and reduce readers’ trust. Nuance and humility have a place in our writing, but we aim to present ourselves as experts and industry thought leaders.

### **Writing/Speaking About Competitors**

We never write negative emails or content regarding our competitors as it does not elevate us. We can show our differences and contrast our advantages while respecting our competitors. In general, we do not mention competitors by name in our writing unless we are using vetted marketing material. 

In most cases, avoid talking about specific competitors altogether. Instead, focus on what sets RainFocus apart in the market at large. Frame competitive advantages in a positive way. The following are just a few of our key differentiators:

* **Complete flexibility.** We can pivot more quickly and more seamlessly than any other competitor on the market. When our clients pivot, they maintain access to all of their data and processes.    
    
* **All-in-one functionality.** Simply put, we do it all. We’ve got everything needed to deliver a successful event from venue sourcing and planning to onsite execution.   
    
* **Advanced personalization.** No other company on the market has an attendee record comparable to ours. Our Attendee Record, combined with our Recommendations Bot and our Targeted Agendas, enables event teams to tailor the attendee experience with minimal effort.   
    
* **Unified events.** Almost every company claims that they can run all of a company’s events from one central platform. However, none can optimize the data of multiple events to the same extent that we can.   
    
* **Consistent experiences.** Our Event Portal is highly intuitive and provides attendees an easy way to manage their event experiences.   
    
* **Top-notch security.** We are an event software company that has achieved ISO27001:2013 certification.   
    
* **Unmatched client care.** We are constantly receiving praise for the amount of attention we show our clients. Our clients feel heard, regard us as friends, and are excited to be a part of our growth.

**Acronyms and Initialisms**

For most acronyms and initialisms (e.g. MAP, SMART goals, CTA, KPI, PVM), spell out the full phrase or name on the first reference, followed by the acronym or initialism in parentheses. For subsequent references, use only the acronym or initialism. If the acronym or initialism is used only once in the document, spell it out and do not include the shortened version. 

Most full phrases and names are written in lowercase, with the exception of “BYOD.”

**Capitalization**  
For headlines, subheads, and similar forms of text, we generally use title case, which calls for capitalizing the first letter of every word except articles, articles and prepositions shorter than four words, and conjunctions. When a preposition functions as part of an adverb phrase, it is capitalized. Words following a hyphen, dash, or colon are capitalized. [This tool can automate your capitalization.](https://capitalizemytitle.com/style/AP/) Double-check for accuracy.

For presentations, capitalization rules may be specific to the event or the template being used. In general, slide titles and other headers (e.g. above tables) appearing within the slide use title case. Bullet points and body copy use standard sentence case.  

* Here’s Why RainFocus Is the Best Platform You Can Buy   
* Are You Using Single Sign-On Technology Yet?  
* Run Face-to-Face Meetings Easily With the New Essential Offering  
* INSIGHT 2022: An Experience in Utah That Attendees Will Never Forget

Job titles and common department names are not capitalized.

* Heather is a proud member of the marketing team.  
* Check out our blog post for some expert predictions from Doug Baird, founder of RainFocus.

In running/body text, do not capitalize words in the middle of sentences for emphasis.

**Incorrect:** We are excited to announce that the Team will be introducing a new Product next year to drive New Client Relationships and bring the Company greater Success.  
**Correct:** We are excited to announce that the team will be introducing a new product next year to drive new client relationships and bring the company greater success.

In modern usage, the following words are not capitalized unless they start the sentence. 

* website (always one word)  
* web  
* homepage  
* internet  
* smartphone  
* online  
* email

**Contractions**  
Contractions are permitted. Use them whenever it feels natural to give your writing an informal tone. 

**Currency**  
When writing about U.S. currency, use the dollar sign before the amount. Include a decimal and number of cents if more than 0\. When writing about other currencies, follow the same format.

* $20  
* $19.99  
* ¥1  
*  €1

**Dates**  
Generally, spell out the day of the week and the month. Abbreviate only if needed for space (e.g. in a graphic or table). 

Use commas after days of the week and years. Do not use ordinal numbers. Use a dash to separate dates showing a span.

* On Friday, January 4, 2023, we will hold a special party for clients.  
* Client party: Fri., Jan. 4  
* INSIGHT will be February 7–9.

Write out all four digits of the year when referring to decades. Do not use an apostrophe.

* The earliest inspiration for this feature comes from 2000s technology.  
* Back in the 1990s, RainFocus had not yet been founded.  

Capitalize the names of months in all uses. When a month is used with a specific date, abbreviate only Jan., Feb., Aug., Sept., Oct., Nov. and Dec. 

Spell out months when using alone, or with a year alone.

* October will be a busy month for many of our clients\!

When a phrase lists only a month and a year, do not separate the year with commas. When a phrase refers to a month, day and year, set off the year with commas.

**Decimals and Fractions**  
Spell out and hyphenate fractions. Use decimal points when a number can’t be easily written out as a fraction, like 1.375 or 47.2.

* Research showed that two-thirds of our users run large-scale events. 

**Geography**  
Spell out all city and state names. Don’t abbreviate city names.

Per AP Style, all cities should be accompanied by their state, with the exception of Atlanta, Baltimore, Boston, Chicago, Cincinnati, Cleveland, Dallas, Denver, Detroit, Honolulu, Houston, Indianapolis, Las Vegas, Los Angeles, Miami, Milwaukee, Minneapolis, New Orleans, New York, Oklahoma City, Philadelphia, Phoenix, Pittsburgh, St. Louis, Salt Lake City, San Antonio, San Diego, San Francisco, Seattle, Washington.

Note that Salt Lake City is included in the list of dateline cities. Therefore, it is not followed by “UT.” 

* Join Us in Salt Lake City for INSIGHT This Year\!

Use “U.S.” for “United States.” 

The following international cities are dateline cities: Amsterdam, Baghdad, Bangkok, Beijing, Beirut, Berlin, Brussels, Cairo, Djibouti, Dublin, Geneva, Gibraltar, Guatemala City, Havana, Helsinki, Hong Kong, Islamabad, Istanbul, Jerusalem, Johannesburg, Kuwait City, London, Luxembourg, Macau, Madrid, Mexico City, Milan, Monaco, Montreal, Moscow, Munich, New Delhi, Panama City, Paris, Prague, Quebec City, Rio de Janeiro, Rome, San Marino, Sao Paulo, Shanghai, Singapore, Stockholm, Sydney, Tokyo, Toronto, Vatican City, Vienna, Zurich.

For other international locations, specify the country (not the continent).

* The local user conference will be held in Nairobi, Kenya.

**Names and Titles**  
The first time you mention a person in writing, refer to them by their first and last names. On all other mentions, refer to them by their last name.

**Numbers**  
Spell out a number when it begins a sentence (or blog post title, headline, etc.). Otherwise, spell out all numbers under 10\. Use numerals for larger numbers. 

* In this guide, we’ll discuss five new ways to make the most of your virtual event.  
* Twelve Expert Predictions From JR Sherman  
* Four Proven Strategies to Engage Exhibitors   
* More than 94 people have downloaded this e-book.

Add a comma or multiple commas for numbers with more than three digits. 

Do not use “K” as an abbreviation for “thousand” in text unless required for space (e.g. in a tweet or graphic). 

* The platform offers 15,349 different options for designing your registration page.   
* More than 30,000 people attended last year’s flagship event.

**Percentages**  
Use the % symbol instead of spelling out "percent."

**Quotations**  
When quoting someone in a blog post or other publication, use the present tense to reference a quotation from an interview. Use the past tense to reference a quotation at an event or in a published statement. 

* “We’re very excited about the future of the company,” says JR Sherman, CEO.  
* “INSIGHT is off to a wonderful start,” said Doug Baird, president, as he addressed the crowd on the first day of the event.

Capitalize the first word in a sentence in a quotation. Always include speaker tags. Place speaker tags between the sentences in a two-sentence quotation.

* “We did the configuration on the back end so that they could just drag and drop content into Targeted Agendas,” Lauren Wolf, event technology program manager at VMware, explained. “This really helped us achieve our goal of reducing 3,000 sessions into something consumable.” 

**Ranges and Spans**

Use “to,” not a hyphen, to indicate a range or span of numbers. Use “between” with “and,” not with “to.” 

* Choose two to three keynote sessions to attend.  
* It typically takes between 20 and 30 days to get this module up and running.

**Telephone Numbers**  
Use parentheses for area codes and no spaces between numbers. For all materials intended for a global audience, include a country code.

* (555) 123-4567  
* \+1 (877) 544-445

**Temperature**  
Use the degree symbol and the capital F abbreviation for Fahrenheit. Use the degree symbol and the capital C abbreviation for Celsius.

* 92°F

**Time**  
Use numerals and “a.m.” or “p.m.”, with a space in between. Include minutes for on-the-hour time. 

* The networking breakfast is scheduled for 7 a.m.  
* Awards ceremony: 7:30 p.m.

Use a dash between times to indicate a time period. 

* 7 a.m.–10:30 p.m.

Specify time zones when writing about an event, a meeting, or anything else requiring registration and scheduling. 

Abbreviate time zones within the continental United States as follows:

* Eastern time: ET  
* Central time: CT  
* Mountain time: MT  
* Pacific time: PT

*Note:* If you include a reference to standard time (e.g. EST) or daylight saving time (e.g. MDT), verify that the event takes place at a point in the year when the indicated time is in effect. 

For example, “July 17 at 10 a.m. MST” is incorrect, as daylight saving time is in effect in July. Either MT or MDT is correct. MT is correct year-round. 

When referring to international time zones, spell them out: Nepal Standard Time, Australian Eastern Time. If a time zone does not have a set name, use its Coordinated Universal Time (UTC) offset.

**URLs and Websites**  
Capitalize the names of websites and web publications. Don’t italicize.  
Avoid spelling out URLs, but when you need to, leave off the “http://www” portion.

**Writing About Other Companies**  
Use the formatting and style of companies’ own names for themselves and their products. Capitalize and punctuate them as they are written on their official website.

* iPhone  
* VMware  
* SAP  
* SK-II

Refer to a company or product as “it,” not “they.”

* Adobe will be holding its biggest conference of the year later this month, and it will offer virtual and in-person attendance options. 

**Text Formatting**  
Use italics to indicate the title of a long work (like a book, movie, or album) or to emphasize a word.

Don’t use underline formatting, and don’t use any combination of italic, bold, caps, and underline formatting.

Left-align text. Never center or right-align unless you are working in an approved specialized branded layout.

Use one space between sentences (not two).

Avoid widows and orphans in your layouts. Ensure all lines, including the final line of each paragraph, contain at least five characters, not including punctuation. 

Avoid placing a hyphenated word at the end of a line. The hyphen can be confused with the automatic hyphen denoting a word that was broken between two lines.

**Punctuation**

**Ampersands**  
Do not use ampersands unless one is part of a company or brand name. Use “and” instead.

* With RainFocus, you can unify your marketing and events.  
* Our newest client is Ben & Jerry’s.

**Apostrophes**  
The apostrophe’s most common use is making a word possessive. Traditionally, if the word already ends in an s and it’s singular, add an ’s. However, adding an apostrophe without the s in the RainFocus possessive has been established as an exception. If the word ends in an s and is plural, add an apostrophe. 

* SAP’s Event Flawlessly Executed Thanks to RainFocus  
* RainFocus’ new Essential offering is designed for meetings and other small events.

Do not use apostrophes to form non-possessive plurals.

* All of the sales team members attended a call to discuss the latest deals.   
* This year, the Shermans will be hosting all employees and their guests at their house for a company Christmas party.  
* Our new messaging targets CMOs and CEOs in several prioritized sectors. 

**Colons**  
Use a colon (rather than an ellipsis, em dash, or comma) to offset a list.

* The RainFocus platform helps you track all kinds of data: attendee data, exhibitor data, housing data, and more. 

Colons can also be used to join two related sentences or phrases. If a complete sentence follows the colon, capitalize the first word.

* This year will present numerous opportunities: Large events are returning, people are excited, and new integrations are planned. 

Do not use a colon after a verb or preposition followed by a series.

* Enhancements to the module include an updated folder view of reports, a new notification setup, and updated action functions.   
* Enhancements to the module include the following: an updated folder view of reports, a new notification setup, and updated action functions.   
* Large events will be held in North America, Europe, and Asia.

**Commas**   
Our style uses serial commas (contrary to AP Style).

* Our platform can help CMOs, event directors, and other business leaders.

Avoid comma splices. Do not join two independent clauses with a comma. Use a semicolon or write two sentences.

* Travis will be speaking at INSIGHT on Tuesday morning; however, he will be happy to meet with any potential new customers afterward.   
* This bug should be fixed right away. Is anyone on the team available?

Use a comma after “e.g.” in a series. Do not use “and” before the last item in the series. Use a comma before “etc.” Do not use “e.g.” and “etc.” in the same series.

* The client will be taking all appropriate precautions at the event (e.g., attendee vaccination, masks, ventilation).   
* You can collect all kinds of attendee data: session attendance, booth visits, etc.

If one or more items in a series contain commas, use semicolons to separate all items in the series.

* The robust platform includes tools for attendee, speaker, and exhibitor management; content delivery; gamification; reporting; engagement scoring; and much more.

Use a comma to set off nonessential clauses. Do not use a comma to set off essential clauses.

* Brian Gates, who lives in Utah, will be holding a special ski outing.

The above clause is nonessential because we already know which Brian is being discussed. It simply adds more information.

* All employees who live in the local area are invited to a special ski outing.

The above clause is essential. It defines which employees are being invited. If the comma were included, the sentence would state (inaccurately) that all employees live in the local area.

* Remote employees, who work out of several different countries around the world, cannot attend the ski outing.

The above clause is nonessential. It adds further description about the remote employees. 

**Additional Uses of Commas**  
Use a comma after an introductory adverb clause, certain introductory words, an introductory participial phrase, or an introductory prepositional phrase. (Some style guides use commas only after a series of introductory prepositional phrases, but AP Style uses a comma after a single introductory prepositional phrase.)

* Although helping a client run a live event can be difficult, seeing the platform used in real life is exciting.  
* No, we cannot accommodate that complex personalization request at this point.   
* In the morning, we will be kicking off INSIGHT.

Use a comma to separate two or more adjectives preceding a noun. Do not use a comma when an adjective modifies the following adjective. Do not use a comma before the final adjective in a series if the adjective is thought of as part of the noun.

* Organizations of all sizes can find success with our powerful, scalable, secure platform.  
* If you have a non-Pixel Android phone, make sure to download the newest security update.  
* We selected a bright pink color as part of the new brand identity.  
* They held a safe in-person event on a cold winter day.

Use a comma to set off appositives, words in direct address, and parenthetical expressions. Do not use a comma after a title or other descriptor that precedes the noun unless the entire phrase is treated as an appositive.

* Annalee, our talented creative director, can lead the design of that asset.  
* We asked graphic designer Korra about her favorite hobbies.  
* The new update will, we hope, improve security and usability. 

Use a comma before the conjunction that joins two independent clauses.

* The configuration will be completed by the client, but the technical account manager will provide guidance as needed.  
* Our Essential offering is designed for small events, so you should consider another option for your largest conference. 

**Dashes**  
Use an em dash (—) with spaces on both sides to offset an aside.  
Use a true em dash, not hyphens (- or \--).

**Ellipses**  
Ellipses (...) can be used to indicate a pause or that you’re trailing off before the end of a thought. Use them sparingly. 

Always use three periods surrounded by spaces. On most computers, this series will automatically format as ellipses. 

**Exclamation Points**  
Use exclamation points sparingly, and never more than one at a time.   
Exclamation points go inside quotation marks. Like periods and question marks, they go outside parentheses when the parenthetical is part of a larger sentence, and inside parentheses when the parenthetical stands alone.

**File Extensions**  
When referring generally to a file extension type, use all uppercase without a period. Add a lowercase s to make plural. Do not use an apostrophe.

* GIF  
* PDFs  
* HTML  
* JPGs

When it refers to a specific file, the filename should be lowercase.

* slowclap.gif  
* RFBenefits.pdf  
* mike-twitter-profile.jpg  
* insight.html

**Hyphens**  
Use a hyphen (-) without spaces on either side to link words into a single phrase. 

* first-time user

Do not hyphenate “-ly” adverbs followed by adjectives.

* The competing event solution is extremely cumbersome and confusing.  
* Our highly scalable platform will work for any event.   
* They held a small socially distanced meeting in 2020 but mostly held virtual events.

*See the “Hyphenation: Frequently Used Terms” section for additional guidance.*  
 

**Periods**

Use a period at the end of a sentence. Do not use a period after a short phrase or incomplete sentence in a bulleted list.

* On-brand content delivery across three different geographical regions, with multi-language support  
* More than 140% increase in sessions scheduled due to insight-driven workflows  
* 50K attendees

Periods go inside quotation marks. They go outside parentheses when the parenthetical is part of a larger sentence, and inside parentheses when the parenthetical stands alone.

* The client said, “That was our best event ever.”  
* Keep attendees in the loop with automated emails and stay in the know with real-time reporting and alerts (available to all clients).  
* For repeatable success, consider our Essential package. (Essential is designed for smaller events and meetings.)

**Question Marks**  
Question marks go inside quotation marks if they’re part of the quotation. Like periods, they go outside parentheses when the parenthetical is part of a larger sentence, and inside parentheses when the parenthetical stands alone.

**Quotation Marks**  
Use quotation marks to refer to words and letters, titles of short works (such as articles and poems), and direct quotations. Use italics for long works. 

Periods and commas go within quotation marks. Question marks within quotations follow logic—if the question mark is part of the quotation, it goes within. If you’re asking a question that ends with a quotation, it goes outside the quotation marks.

Use single quotation marks for quotations within quotations.

Do not use quotation marks to denote words that are not part of a quotation (e.g. a colloquial or emphasized word).

**Incorrect:** The networking session will offer “cool” prizes to our most active participants.   
**Incorrect:** This security challenge will require strict cooperation from “everyone.”

**Semicolons**  
Semicolons can be used to join two independent clauses. They are also used in a series of items when at least one item in the series contains a comma.

* Our customer care team is here to help; contact them with any questions.  
* Use the platform for attendee, speaker, and exhibitor management; content delivery; gamification; reporting; engagement scoring; and much more.

**Slashes**  
Do not place spaces around slashes.

* All of our Tuesday/Thursday meetings will be canceled next week.  
* This campaign targets director/executive personas.

**Hyphenation: Frequently Used Terms**

General guidance: Many phrases with this construction are hyphenated or written as one word only when used as an adjective or noun. 

For additional clarity, examples are provided for several terms that are commonly used in RainFocus materials.

**On brand, on-brand**  
Hyphenate only when the phrase is used as an adjective. 

* The team can create beautiful on-brand templates for you.   
* We will ensure all of your event materials are on brand.


**On demand, on-demand**  
Hyphenate only when the phrase is used as an adjective. 

* All of the sessions will be available to watch on demand for two weeks after the event.  
* Click here to view our full library of on-demand videos from the conference\!

*Note:* “OnDemand” is a brand name. It is *never* correct for our usage. “Ondemand” is also never correct.

**In person, in-person**  
Hyphenate only when the phrase is used as an adjective placed before the noun.

* Unfortunately, the big annual gala will not be held in person this year.  
* Depending on final registration numbers, we may see 850 people at our in-person event next week.  
* The networking portion of the conference is exclusively in person.

*Note:* We use “in person” and “in-person” (rather than “physical”) to describe non-virtual events.

**Log in, log-in, login**  
Write as one word only for adjective or noun usage. Write as two words as a verb. “Log in to” is three words; “log into” is incorrect. “Log-in” is no longer used.

* The virtual attendees may have trouble logging in to their portal today.   
* Your technical account manager will make sure your login page is built in time.

**Real-time, in real time**  
Hyphenate only when the phrase is used as an adjective. 

* Our clients benefit from access to real-time data within the platform.  
* Our clients can access their data in real time.

**Set up, setup**  
Write the verb as two words. Write the noun or adjective as one word. 

* We will provide time for exhibitors to set up their booths tomorrow evening.   
* The mobile app allows for quick and easy setup of your event. 

**Sign up, sign-up**  
Hyphenate only when the phrase is used as an adjective. 

* Click here to sign up for the new RainFocus newsletter.   
* You can access a copy of your networking reception sign-up form in this part of the platform. 

**Check in, check-in**  
Hyphenate only when the phrase is used as an adjective.

* Check in at several Utah landmarks to win prizes in our INSIGHT game.  
* Who is managing the check-in desk on Wednesday morning? 

**Check out, checkout**   
Write the verb as two words. Write the noun or adjective as one word. 

* Attendees will have trouble checking out on the hotel registration page if their credit card information is entered incorrectly.  
* Check out the amazing new features in our latest product update\!  
* We have made numerous improvements to your checkout experience during your event ticket purchase.

**On-site**  
Official guidance differs from the usual pattern. “On-site” should be used in all adjectival and adverbial instances. 

* Ashleigh will be on-site next week, so feel free to book a meeting with her.  
* The on-site portion of the meeting will include a free lunch for attendees.

**Selected Grammar Guidelines**

**Subject/Verb Agreement**

Singular subjects take singular verbs. Plural subjects take plural verbs. The number of the subject is not changed by a phrase following the subject. 

The following common words are singular: Each, either, neither, one, no one, everyone, anyone, someone, anybody, somebody, everybody.

**Incorrect:** The features of our new offering, which will be released next year, is impressive.  
**Correct:** The features of our new offering, which will be released next year, are impressive. 

**Incorrect:** Each of the executives at the event were eager to sign up for the VIP roundtable.  
**Correct:** Each of the executives at the event was eager to sign up for the VIP roundtable.

**Incorrect:** Neither Doug nor JR have brought enough extra business cards to this popular meeting.  
**Correct:** Neither Doug nor JR has brought enough extra business cards to this popular meeting.

**Pronouns**

**Objective vs. Nominative**  
Use nominative pronouns for subjects of sentences. Use objective pronouns for objects of sentences. 

Use “myself” only as an intensifier or reflexive, or with the meaning “my true self.” Do not use “myself” as a substitute for “me” or “I” when “me” or “I” would be the correct pronoun.

**Incorrect:** If you have any doughnuts, you can give them to Wayne or myself.   
**Incorrect:** If you have any doughnuts, you can give them Wayne or I.   
**Incorrect:** Mike and myself will be serving doughnuts in the break room.

**Correct:** If you have any doughnuts, you can give them to Wayne or me.  
**Correct:** Mike and I will be serving doughnuts in the break room.   
**Correct:** The providers of doughnuts are Mike and I.   
**Correct:** Mike and Wayne ate all the doughnuts; I saw it myself\!

**“Who” vs. “Whom”**  
“Who” and “whom” often cause confusion. “Who” is a nominative pronoun that is used in where “he,” “she,” “I,” and similar pronouns, would be used. “Whom” is a subjective pronoun (as are “her,” “them,” “me,” etc.). Although they are used interchangeably in informal speech, writers often incorrectly use “whom” in writing in an effort to be formal.

To test whether “who” or “whom” is correct, ask whether you would use a nominative pronoun (e.g., he, she) or a subjective pronoun (e.g., her, him). If the nominative pronoun is correct, use “who” or a related form. If the subjective pronoun is correct, use “whom” or a related form. 

**Incorrect:** Your team will work with a dedicated solution consultant, whom is here to help configure the platform to suit your needs.   
**Correct:** Your team will work with a dedicated solution consultant, who is here to help configure the platform to suit your needs.   
(“**She** is here to help configure the platform to suit your needs.”)

**Incorrect:** The keynote speaker, who we have asked to stay for an audience Q\&A, will deliver groundbreaking thought leadership.  
**Correct:** The keynote speaker, whom we have asked to stay for an audience Q\&A, will deliver groundbreaking thought leadership.   
(“We have asked **him** to stay for an audience Q\&A.”)

**Incorrect:** Whom is the correct account manager to contact?   
**Correct:** Who is the correct account manager to contact?   
(“**She** is the correct account manager to contact.”)

**Incorrect:** You can give your company’s Innovation Award to whomever has played a major role in event execution.  
**Correct:** You can give your company’s Innovation Award to whoever has played a major role in event execution.  
(“**He** has played a major role in event execution.”)

In this example, the phrase “whoever has played a major role in event execution” is the object of the preposition. To test whether “who” or “whom” is correct, ask whether you would use a nominative pronoun (e.g.  “He has played a major role in event execution”) or a subjective pronoun (e.g. “Him has played a major role in event execution”) in the phrase. 

Do not use “that” to refer to people. Use “who” or “whom” as appropriate.

**Incorrect:** Determine which attendees are eligible for networking access, and allow attendees that are participating to opt in and create customized profiles.  
**Correct:** Determine which attendees are eligible for networking access, and allow attendees who are participating to opt in and create customized profiles.

**Singular vs. Plural Pronouns**  
If your subject’s gender is unknown or irrelevant (or if the subject uses “they/them/their” as their pronouns) use “they,” “them,” and “their” as a singular pronoun. Contrary to myth, it has a long history of grammatically correct usage. 

Avoid defaulting to “he” or “she” or using the awkward “she/he” construction. Use “he/him/his” and “she/her/her” pronouns as appropriate. Avoid using “one” as a pronoun. 

* Each attendee can select their preferred dietary option for the grab-and-go lunch.  
* Our special mystery keynote speaker will share their top strategies for navigating hybrid events.   
* In an interview, Doug explained that he expects his new idea for an offering to be revealed soon.   
* Irene says she will be closing a huge contract with a major client next month.

**Misplaced and Dangling Modifiers** 

The goal of clear writing is to allow the reader to focus on the message rather than trying to decipher what is being said. A misplaced or dangling modifier can mislead readers or force them to reread the sentence to understand its meaning. 

Generally, placing a modifier close to the word it modifies makes a sentence clearer. A sentence with a dangling modifier does not contain the word it modifies. To correct a dangling modifier, reword the sentence to include the word it modifies. 

**Misplaced Modifiers**

**Incorrect:** By offering in-person or virtual attendance tickets to the event, attendees can select the option they prefer.  
(Attendees are not offering in-person or virtual event attendance tickets.)  
**Correct:** By offering in-person or virtual attendance tickets to the event, we allow attendees to select the option they prefer.

**Incorrect:** We will be holding a meeting to discuss the specifics of hosting Adobe’s big event in the Lehi office.  
(The event will not be held in the Lehi office.)  
**Correct:** We will be holding a meeting in the Lehi office to discuss the specifics of hosting Adobe’s big event. 

**Incorrect:** As a successful event software company, you can be sure your events will be run efficiently with RainFocus.  
(The “you” is not the successful event software company.)  
**Correct:** As a successful event software company, RainFocus ensures your events will be run efficiently.  
   
**Dangling Modifiers**

**Incorrect:** Getting ready for the important meeting with new investors, Mike’s dress shirt suddenly tore.  
(The shirt was not getting ready for the meeting.)  
**Correct:** Getting ready for the important meeting with new investors, Mike saw his dress shirt had suddenly torn. 

**Incorrect:** While running through the halls of the Moscone Center to reach the registration desk, the badge scanner fell out of her hands.   
(The badge scanner was not running.)  
**Correct:** While running through the halls of the Moscone Center to reach the registration desk, she dropped the badge scanner.  

**Parallelism**

In a parallel sentence, two or more sentence elements of equal rank are similarly expressed. Parallel construction is important for clarity and smoothness of writing. 

Express parallel ideas in the same grammatical form.

**Incorrect:** We are looking for a new technical account manager who is hardworking, patient, and loves to help clients.  
**Correct:** We are looking for a new technical account manager who is hardworking, patient, and helpful.

**Incorrect:** The team will consider event size, location, and whether attendees are primarily CMOs.  
**Incorrect:** The team will consider event size, location, as well as whether attendees are primarily CMOs.   
**Correct:** The team will consider event size, location, and attendee type.  
**Correct:** The team will consider event size and location, as well as whether attendees are primarily CMOs. 

Place correlative conjunctions immediately before parallel terms.

**Incorrect:** We not only enjoyed a record-setting year for deals closed but also client satisfaction scores.  
**Correct:** We enjoyed a record-setting year not only for deals closed but also for client satisfaction scores. 

Repeat an article, preposition, or pronoun as necessary to make the meaning clear.

**Incorrect:** The pandemic was a smaller challenge for us than competitors.  
**Correct:** The pandemic was a smaller challenge for us than for our competitors.  
**Correct (different meaning):** The pandemic was a smaller challenge for us than our competitors were. 

Include in the second part of a parallel construction all words necessary to make the construction complete.

**Incorrect:** The integrations and personalization options for our platform are better than Hopin.  
**Correct:** The integrations and personalization options for our platform are better than those of Hopin.  
**Correct:** The integrations and personalization options for our platform are better than Hopin’s integrations and personalization options.
